Starting a Data Career the Hard Way - And Why I’m Doing It Anyway

I’ve been working full‑time while trying to navigate the pretty battered job market my generation graduated into, and it’s forced me to do a lot of reflecting.

I went to university to study sport because, honestly, I didn’t know what else to do with my life. I’d worked all through the lockdowns while everyone else was stuck inside, so I felt like I had a head start. Then came A‑levels — which my sixth form still made us sit despite barely any in‑person teaching, while other colleges handed out predicted grades — and with another lockdown fading into the past, I decided to go to uni.

Uni was a mix of everything: meeting people from all sorts of backgrounds (my flat of eight had only three 18‑year‑olds), partying with the money I’d saved from working, and slowly figuring out who I was. But somewhere in between all that, something clicked. I’d spend hours in the library working on the results sections of assignments in my first and second years and finish them in a single four‑hour sitting, while others took weeks. I realised I didn’t mind the work — in fact, I found it interesting.

So when a placement in Sports Performance Data Analysis came up, I jumped at it. It was the first time I saw how data could actually answer real questions, not just fill a spreadsheet. My dissertation — “What is the optimal dosage and timing of curcumin supplementation in athletes for reducing exercise‑induced muscle damage?” — pushed that even further. I developed proper research, data analysis, and reporting skills, and I enjoyed the process far more than I expected.

But here’s the thing about a Sport & Exercise Science degree: if you want to specialise — physio, nutrition, strength & conditioning — you need a master’s and a stack of accreditations. And I knew I didn’t want to be a coach. What I did know was that the analytical side of my degree was the part I enjoyed most. The part I was good at. The part that made sense.

The Idea

So now, having graduated, I’m pivoting into pure data analysis. It’s not the path I planned, but it’s the one that feels right. And honestly, very few people end up in data because they mapped it out from age 16. Most of us arrive here sideways — through curiosity, through problem‑solving, through realising we enjoy the work more than we expected.

I’m beginning this journey in the same position a lot of people find themselves in: working full‑time, trying to pivot into data analysis, and squeezing learning into evenings, weekends, and whatever scraps of energy are left after the day job. It’s not glamorous, and it’s definitely not the “quit your job and study full‑time” narrative you see online. But it’s real — and it’s the path I’m taking.

I’m writing this because I want to document the process honestly. Not the polished “I became a data analyst in 30 days” version, but the actual grind: the late‑night SQL practice, the Excel formulas that break for no reason, the dashboards that look awful before they look good, and the constant balancing act between improving my skills and applying for roles that feel just slightly out of reach.

My goal isn’t just to learn data tools. It’s to build a portfolio that proves I can think like an analyst — solve problems, find insights, and tell a story with data. I’ve already been experimenting with Excel, Power BI, and data cleaning, but this post marks the moment I commit to a clear, structured plan. By putting it here, publicly, I’m holding myself accountable and giving myself something to look back on as I progress. My Data Analysis Roadmap

This is the plan I’m following — realistic, chronological, and built around full‑time work. It’s here so I can track my progress, reflect on it, and stay accountable.

Phase 1 — Excel (2–4 weeks)

  • Complete an advanced Excel course

  • Build one flagship Excel project

  • Upload it to this website

Phase 2 — SQL (4–6 weeks)

  • Datacamp for fundamentals

  • Khan Academy for unguided practice

  • Build:

    • Two SQL case studies
    • A small SQL‑only project to show query logic and business thinking

  • Then, once Power BI is learned, return to SQL to build a full end‑to‑end project

Phase 3 — Power BI (6–8 weeks)

  • Complete the Datacamp Power BI track

    • Learn:

      • Data modelling

      • Power Query

      • DAX

      • Dashboard design

      • Build a multi‑page dashboard project

  • Then combine SQL + Power BI into one end‑to‑end project:

    • Clean and query data in SQL

    • Export results

    • Build a Power BI model + dashboard from your SQL output

    • Sit the PL‑300 exam when confident

Phase 4 — Optional Extras

  • Tableau (if jobs call for it)

  • Python (down the line for advanced querying)

This is day one — before the projects, before the certification, before the job offer. I want to show the whole journey, not just the highlight reel. If you’re reading this, you’re seeing the start of something I’m genuinely committed to building, one evening and weekend at a time.
